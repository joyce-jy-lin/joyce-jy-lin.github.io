Research

My research program investigates how environmental and social exposures across the life course influence trajectories of aging and chronic disease.

Research vision: to identify sensitive windows, pathways, and intervention points that can promote healthy aging and reduce environmental health inequities.

Developmental origins of aging

I study how exposures during prenatal, childhood, and early-life periods shape health decades later. My current work leverages the St. Louis Baby Tooth - Later Life Health Study, which uses baby teeth to reconstruct early-life lead exposure across prenatal and early postnatal windows and links these exposures to adult health outcomes.

Environmental exposures and chronic disease

My work examines how metals and other environmental toxicants contribute to aging-related outcomes including mental health, cognitive function, obesity, and cardiometabolic disease. I am especially interested in exposure timing, mixtures, and heterogeneity in susceptibility.

Social and environmental determinants of healthy aging

Environmental exposures do not occur in isolation. I investigate how psychosocial resources, stress, and social environments may modify or mediate the health impacts of toxic exposures across the life course.

Biological pathways and intervention points

My future research will integrate epidemiologic methods with measures of cardiometabolic health, epigenetic signatures, and other biological pathways to understand how early-life environmental exposures become embodied across decades.

Biomonitoring and exposure assessment

My methodological foundation is in exposure science and biomonitoring. I have developed and applied methods using toenails, baby teeth, environmental monitoring, and low-cost sensors to characterize chemical exposures in large epidemiologic and community-engaged studies.
