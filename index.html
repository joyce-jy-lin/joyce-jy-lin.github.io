Life-Course Determinants of Healthy Aging and Chronic Disease

My research investigates how environmental and social exposures across the life course shape trajectories of healthy aging and chronic disease.

I integrate environmental epidemiology, exposure science, biomonitoring, and causal inference methods to understand how experiences during sensitive developmental periods influence health decades later. My work seeks to identify biological and social pathways linking environmental exposures to aging-related outcomes and uncover opportunities for prevention across the lifespan.

Research Areas

Developmental Origins of Healthy Aging

Investigating how environmental exposures during prenatal, childhood, and adolescent periods shape health trajectories across adulthood.

Environmental and Social Determinants of Aging

Understanding how chemical exposures interact with psychosocial and structural factors to influence aging-related outcomes.

Mechanisms Linking Exposure and Disease

Identifying biological and behavioral pathways, including mental health, cardiometabolic health, and epigenetic processes.

Biomonitoring and Exposure Assessment

Developing and applying biomarkers to characterize environmental exposures across the life course.

Research Vision

My long-term goal is to understand how environmental and social experiences across the life course contribute to healthy aging and chronic disease. By identifying sensitive windows of vulnerability, mechanisms of disease development, and opportunities for intervention, my research aims to inform strategies that promote healthy aging and reduce environmental health disparities.
